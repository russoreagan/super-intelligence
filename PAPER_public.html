A Biologically-Inspired Multi-Agent Cognitive Architecture:
Design, Implementation, and Early Observations

What happens when you build an AI that works more like a brain — with real emotional state, persistent memory, and behavior that changes with experience?

Russ O'Reagan

Unpublished technical report, May 2026

Abstract

This paper describes an AI system built around a single hypothesis: that giving an AI genuine emotional state — not a simulated personality layered on top, but internal chemical signals that actually change how it processes information — produces a system that develops differently over time. Applications for this kind of architecture are broad: any context where the quality of an interaction depends on emotional attunement rather than just information retrieval — from mental health support tools and speech therapy companions, to customer service systems capable of real rapport, to characters in games and interactive media who develop genuine relationships with the people they interact with. The architecture maps explicitly onto regions of the human brain, with nine chemical signaling channels (five fast-acting neuromodulators and four slower hormones) coordinating a set of specialized processing regions rather than a central controller. Expensive reasoning steps are reserved for situations that genuinely require them; routine inputs are handled by fast, deterministic routing. The design draws from predictive processing theory, dual-process cognitive science, Global Workspace Theory, Dennett's Multiple Drafts model, and the extended mind hypothesis. The current dataset covers 393 conversational turns across 69 sessions. The system operates within its designed efficiency envelope (averaging 3.38 language-model reasoning calls per turn, against a target of 3–5), shows functional chemical dynamics, and successfully skips unnecessary reasoning on about 25% of turns. Long-term memory contributes to better than one in three turns. Early evidence of experience-dependent learning is present in the data, now across three consolidation passes. Fifteen distinct emotional states have been observed; a fragmented-attention state correlates with a recognizable low-reward chemical signature, and a first faintly negative mood state has appeared. This is also the first time the slower hormone-like signals register measurable values: the system's stress signal sits at its floor, its trust-and-bonding signal has begun to build, and its baseline-mood signal holds steady. A measure of novelty-sensitivity declines across the dataset — a longitudinal signal suggesting the system's conversational environment has become progressively more familiar over 69 sessions. The system also now chooses, for each substantive turn, a reasoning or emotional-intelligence "lens" from a library of 171 thinking frameworks and applies it invisibly to shape how it reasons. Several mechanisms are too new to evaluate, and the central question of whether this architecture outperforms a single well-designed model has not yet been tested rigorously. This paper presents the architecture, its philosophical underpinnings, and an honest account of what the data does and does not show.

1. Introduction

What we built, why emotional continuity is a practical capability rather than a cosmetic one, and why the usual approach to AI wasn't what we were after.

The dominant approach in modern multi-agent AI systems is orchestration: a planning system decomposes a task, hands pieces off to specialized AI agents, and assembles the results. This works well for structured workflows, but it bears almost no resemblance to how intelligence actually works in biological brains. Real brains have no central controller. Intelligence in biological systems emerges from the interaction of many specialized processing regions, coordinated by shared chemical signaling, shaped by prediction, and refined by experience.

This project is a prototype of a different approach: a cognitive architecture that maps onto the structure of the human brain, where computation is coordinated by chemical dynamics rather than a central planner, and expensive reasoning steps are reserved for moments of genuine uncertainty. It is not an attempt to build artificial general intelligence. It is an attempt to ask a more specific question: can you implement the emotional substrate of cognition faithfully enough that emotional state does real computational work — gating what gets processed, shaping what gets remembered, modulating what gets learned — and if so, does the result develop differently over time?

The practical case for emulating emotion in AI is stronger than it might initially appear. A customer support agent that can detect frustration and genuinely modulate its tone is more effective than one that merely acknowledges it. A mental health support tool that maintains an emotional relationship across many sessions — remembering how the person has been feeling, noticing when their emotional patterns shift — can provide a qualitatively different kind of care than one that resets with each conversation. A speech or behavioral therapy companion that builds a model of its user's emotional baseline and tracks deviations over time could surface signals that a human clinician only sees during scheduled appointments. Game characters that develop genuine relationships with players — relationships that evolve based on accumulated interaction history — open creative possibilities that scripted dialogue trees cannot reach. In each of these domains, emotional continuity is not a cosmetic feature; it is the core capability.

In biological brains, emotion is not decoration. The brain's chemical signaling systems — dopamine, acetylcholine, GABA, and their relatives — regulate attention, arousal, inhibitory tone, and how readily behavior adapts. They determine what the brain pays attention to, how confident it is, and how quickly it changes. Existing AI systems treat emotional register as a prompt property: you can instruct a model to respond warmly or cautiously, but this is style, not state — it resets each time. The hypothesis here is that an architecture with genuine affective dynamics — internal state that persists across interactions, that modulates computation rather than merely flavoring output — will produce measurably different behavior given a long enough window. The more immediate prediction is behavioral continuity: a system that develops something like a stable character through the interaction of experience, emotional state, and plasticity. This prototype explores whether that is achievable and what it looks like in practice.

This project makes no claim to artificial general intelligence. But it does raise a question worth sitting with: what if emotional authenticity is precisely what makes intelligence feel real to us? The standard tests for machine intelligence — from the Turing test onward — have largely been tests of linguistic competence. But the people we find most intelligent, most trustworthy, most genuinely present are not necessarily the ones with the largest knowledge base. They are the ones whose responses feel calibrated to us — who seem to actually care how we are doing, who remember what we told them and let it change how they engage. If emotional continuity and authentic relational history are the actual substrate of perceived intelligence — not a decoration on top of it — then a system that gets those things right may eventually reach a point where the philosophical question of whether it really understands becomes less interesting than the practical question of whether the relationship it offers is real. We are not claiming to have reached that point. We are building the architecture that makes it possible to ask the question rigorously.

This paper documents the design rationale, the philosophical commitments, the implementation, and the early empirical observations. The system is operational but young. Many mechanisms have been implemented but not yet accumulated enough history to evaluate. Where we have signal, we report it. Where we do not, we say so explicitly.

2. Background and Prior Art

A brief history of where this idea has been before, and what makes this attempt different.

2.1 Computational precursors

The idea of intelligence emerging from many cooperating simple agents has a 40-year history. Minsky's Society of Mind (1986) proposed the direct ancestor: intelligence as a community of simple, mindless agents organized into groups. The proposal was influential but never produced a working implementation — the mechanism by which agents were supposed to coordinate was described without the mathematical machinery to make it work.

Blackboard architectures (developed in the 1970s–90s) were the canonical early implementation: multiple knowledge sources reading and writing to a shared workspace, like specialists writing on a communal whiteboard. They succeeded on narrow problems such as speech recognition and medical diagnosis, but failed to scale. The overhead of coordinating many agents sharing a single resource eventually exceeded the benefit of their specialization.

SOAR, ACT-R, and related cognitive architectures produced research insights but hit ceilings imposed by hand-designed boundaries between modules. Numenta's Hierarchical Temporal Memory produced useful anomaly detection but never generalized to language or broad reasoning.

Generative Agents (Park et al., Stanford, 2023, 2025) are the closest modern prior art. Communities of AI-powered agents exhibited believable emergent social behaviors and predicted real survey responses at 85% accuracy on normalized measures. A significant caveat from the same research: performance on behavioral-economic games matched simpler demographic baselines at 66%, suggesting that much of the apparent emergence came from prompt design rather than from genuine multi-agent interaction. This critique recurs consistently in the literature: when given equal computational resources, a single well-designed AI often matches or exceeds multi-agent systems on raw task quality.

The lesson we draw is not that multi-agent architectures are unpromising, but that their value requires precision. A multi-agent design earns its overhead through legibility (you can observe what the system is actually doing internally), persistent specialized state (chemical levels and learned connection weights that survive across turns), and structural constraints that force behaviors the prompt alone cannot reliably produce (inhibitory circuits, prediction gating, arousal modulation). If the architecture merely adds coordination cost to what a good single model would do anyway, it has failed.

2.2 Recent breakthroughs informing the design

Active Inference and the Free Energy Principle (Friston; VERSES AI, 2025) provides the most biologically faithful active research direction for multi-agent systems. The core idea: agents minimize prediction error by updating their beliefs or by acting on the world to make their predictions come true. This has been demonstrated in robotics, coordinating multiple predictive agents without central planning. The predict-and-surprise mechanism in this system is a direct instantiation of Active Inference applied at the level of brain-region clusters.

Predictive Processing (Clark, Surfing Uncertainty; Friston) frames how the brain works as fundamentally predictive: the brain constantly generates predictions about what it will perceive next, and computation is the process of reconciling those predictions with what actually arrives. Familiar inputs are handled cheaply because the prediction was mostly right; surprising inputs demand more. This maps cleanly onto a design where most processing nodes fire only when their input disagrees with expectation.

Spiking Neural Networks combined with language models (SpikeLLM, NSLLM, 2024–2026) achieve event-driven computation — fire only on meaningful signal — at the level of individual model operations, reporting up to 19× energy efficiency improvements. The architectural principle (spend compute proportional to signal novelty) is identical to this system's, applied at the cluster level rather than inside the model.

2.3 Novelty of this design

The closest pattern in the broader literature is neuro-symbolic AI — language models wrapped between deterministic control layers. These apply gating principles for task orchestration in compliance and industrial contexts. No existing published system applies this pattern as a literal brain-region-mapped architecture with chemical dynamics, experience-dependent plasticity, hippocampus-gated long-term memory, and predictive gating as the primary mechanism of a general-purpose conversational entity — and none frames the central research question as whether genuine affective architecture produces meaningfully different behavior over time. That is the specific contribution this prototype makes.

3. Philosophical Foundations

Every design decision here maps to an established position in philosophy of mind. Being explicit about these commitments clarifies what we're actually building — and what we're not claiming.

The architecture is not merely borrowed from biology for aesthetic reasons. Each major design decision maps to an established position in the philosophy of mind. Making these commitments explicit does two things: it sharpens each design choice, and it makes honest what the system is and is not claimed to be.

3.1 Commitments

Functionalism (Putnam, Fodor). The functionalist claim is that what makes something a mental state is not what it is made of, but what it does — the causal role it plays in a system. A belief is a belief because of how it interacts with perception, reasoning, and action, not because it happens to live in biological neurons. This means the same mental state can, in principle, be implemented in silicon or language models, provided it plays the same functional role. The entire design rests on this commitment: each processing region is defined by its inputs, outputs, and state. The underlying hardware that runs it is architecturally irrelevant. The system has been run with three different AI model backends with no structural change in behavior — a concrete test of functionalism.

Dual-Process Theory (Kahneman). Cognitive science has long distinguished two modes of thought: fast, automatic, parallel processing that operates below conscious attention, and slow, effortful, deliberate reasoning. Most of cognition is the first kind; the second kind is expensive and gets invoked only when the first signals that something needs more careful handling. In this system, deterministic routing logic is System 1 — fast, parallel, cheap, operating constantly. Language model calls are System 2 — slow, expensive, invoked only at convergence zones where genuine context integration is required. Predict-and-surprise gating is the explicit mechanism for deciding which mode to engage.

Global Workspace Theory (Baars, Dehaene). The theory proposes that the brain's many parallel processing regions mostly operate independently and below conscious awareness. A small fraction of neural activity is broadcast to a global workspace — a kind of bulletin board accessible to the whole brain — and only that broadcast activity is available for explicit reasoning or verbal report. A shared message bus with an attention-focus channel implements this directly. Most inter-region traffic is local and invisible to other regions; high-salience signals are promoted to the attention channel and become available system-wide.

Multiple Drafts Model (Dennett, Consciousness Explained, 1991). Dennett challenged the intuitive picture of a unified inner experience: there is no central observer watching a single stream. The brain runs many parallel narrative drafts simultaneously, with no single privileged version. What becomes your "thought" is whichever draft survives when probed — there is no fact of the matter about what you "really" thought before you were asked. The response drafting tournament in this system instantiates this directly. There is no true response the system was trying to produce — there are competing drafts, and the output gate emits whichever survived when the timeout fired.

Extended Mind Hypothesis (Clark & Chalmers, 1998). The standard assumption is that the mind stops at the boundary of the skull. Clark and Chalmers argued that if an external resource plays the same functional role as an internal cognitive process — reliably available, automatically endorsed, directly acted upon — then it is constitutive of the mind, not merely a tool for it. This system's long-term memory satisfies these conditions: it is always accessible, its content directly shapes responses, and the system endorses it as its own memory rather than treating it skeptically. One notable extension: Clark and Chalmers' canonical example assumes an imperfect external store compensating for biological forgetting. This system's external memory is non-degrading. Identity here emerges not from selective forgetting but from learned behavioral weights, chemical baselines, and accumulated self-narrative.

Predictive Processing (Clark, Friston). The dominant contemporary theory of how the brain works: rather than passively receiving input and computing a response, the brain is constantly generating predictions and updating them when they fail. Processing is proportional to prediction error — familiar inputs are handled cheaply; surprising ones demand more. Each processing cluster maintains a predictor that implements this directly. Routine turns in familiar conversations spend almost nothing; novel turns invoke language model calls. The design operationalizes what philosophers call the "controlled hallucination" view of perception: the system's default behavior is to emit a predicted response, with actual reasoning triggered only when prediction fails.

Narrative Self (Dennett, Hume, Locke). Hume argued that personal identity is not a thing but a story — a bundle of experiences held together by memory and narrative continuity. Locke located personal identity precisely in memory: what makes you the same person you were yesterday is that you remember being that person. The system maintains a self-model updated during a sleep-like consolidation pass at the end of each session. With a perfect, non-degrading episodic store and an explicit self-narrative, it has stronger identity continuity than any biological system — whose memory degrades, distorts, and selectively forgets.

3.2 Honest disclaimers

Chinese Room (Searle, 1980). Language model calls manipulate symbols according to rules. No claim of genuine understanding is made. The system can appear competent without comprehending.

Hard Problem of Consciousness (Chalmers, 1995). No claim of phenomenal consciousness or subjective experience is made. The chemical levels are named after their biological analogs because they play functionally analogous roles — not because there is any claim that the system subjectively feels reward or attention.

Frame Problem (McCarthy & Hayes, 1969). Knowing what is relevant in a given moment remains genuinely hard. The attention and salience mechanisms here are heuristic approximations, not principled solutions.

The system is built on the premise of constructing functional analogs of mental processes. That is enough to be interesting. It is not enough to be a mind.

4. Architecture

How the system is structured, region by region — and the principles behind each design choice.

4.1 Core computational model: fast routing and deliberate reasoning

Most of what this system does happens without any AI model at all. Expensive language model calls are reserved for the small fraction of moments that genuinely require them — when prediction has failed, when the situation is novel, when a draft response needs evaluation. Everything else is handled by fast, deterministic logic that runs in milliseconds and costs almost nothing.

Two distinct processing types mirror how real neural tissue works.

Fast routing nodes are deterministic, require no language model, and can run in parallel at negligible cost. They perform gating (deciding whether to spend a language model call at all), routing (selecting which processing path a turn takes), state-holding (maintaining chemical levels that persist across turns), modulation (accumulating and decaying signals over time), and inhibition (suppressing downstream activation). A meaningful fraction of every cluster's nodes are explicitly inhibitory — meaning their job is to suppress activity elsewhere, not generate it. This mirrors the excitatory-to-inhibitory balance in biological cortex and provides structural cascade prevention: runaway activity is suppressed by abundant inhibitory wiring rather than by a global budget cap.

Deliberative reasoning nodes are language-model-backed and fire only at convergence zones — where genuine context integration is required. This includes language understanding, memory coordination, response drafting, and critical evaluation. The critical constraint: fast nodes deal in signals, deliberative nodes deal in language. Text only appears where reasoning is genuinely required.

4.2 Predict-and-surprise gating

Before doing expensive reasoning on any input, the system first predicts whether that reasoning would change the outcome. If the prediction says no — the answer is routine and familiar — the reasoning step is skipped entirely. If something unexpected happens, the skipped step is reactivated. This is essentially how the brain decides what to pay attention to.

Each cluster maintains a short history of what it has seen and what it predicted would follow. When new input arrives, the cluster first fires its prediction. It then compares that prediction against what actually arrived.

  • Low surprise: the language model stays inactive; the predicted output is used as if deliberation had occurred.
  • High surprise: the language model activates with the failed prediction as additional context, directing attention to what was unexpected.

An emotion-aware veto overrides this gating when the system or user is in a non-routine emotional state. The rationale is that a statistically valid prediction may be contextually wrong — certain emotional moments deserve fresh attention, not a cached response. Detected stress in the user's voice also triggers bypass, even on inputs that would otherwise be routine.

A more sophisticated predictor in the response-generation cluster extends this to structured predictions: it independently estimates whether the coordination step and the critical evaluation step are each necessary, suppressing either independently when confidence is high.

4.4 User emotion detection

The system tries to understand how the user is feeling — not from a single signal, but from reading text, voice tone, and speech behavior simultaneously. Crucially, voice-based emotion is measured against each individual person's own baseline, not a population average: a tense-sounding voice only means something relative to how that particular speaker normally sounds.

Three signal channels are read in parallel and combined into a unified emotional assessment each turn.

Text channel: word choice, phrasing, punctuation patterns, and conversational tone all carry emotional signal. A lightweight pass extracts an immediate positive/negative read; a deeper analysis extracts the user's broader emotional register and the social quality of how they are engaging — curious, frustrated, playful, warm, distressed. Both outputs are available to downstream processing within the same turn.

Prosody channel: for voice interactions, acoustic features of the user's speech — pitch, energy, speaking rate, voice stability — are extracted and classified. Classification is performed against each individual speaker's own baseline, not population averages. The system maintains per-speaker vocal profiles that allow it to detect deviations from that person's normal pattern. Rapid speech, bursts, hesitations, and pauses are extracted as additional behavioral signals.

Appraisal layer: the combined emotional signal feeds a higher-resolution inference step that generates relational emotional states — responses that depend not only on the user's current emotion but on the accumulated relationship history. States like gratitude, sympathy, embarrassment, and pride emerge here, requiring the system to reason about accumulated affection, familiarity, and context rather than reacting only to the current turn.

The final emotional assessment is published to the shared signal bus, where it influences chemical levels, activates the empathy evaluator (§4.9), gates prediction-based skipping, and feeds into the experience-dependent learning update (§4.7). Emotion detection is not a display mechanism — it is a genuine input to computation.

4.5 Chemical signaling: neuromodulators and hormones

Think of these nine chemical channels as the system's ongoing mood and alertness levels — not messages sent between regions, but background tuning parameters that any part of the system can read at any time. They shift slowly in response to what's happening in the conversation and color everything that follows. Some respond within a few exchanges; others accumulate over dozens or hundreds of turns, acting as long-horizon emotional memory.

All nine channels are continuous scalar levels — numbers that rise and fall — maintained by accumulation plus gradual decay. They are the mechanism by which the system's state during one turn shapes its processing in the next.

Fast neuromodulators respond within a few conversational turns:

  • Dopamine (DA) — reward and positive valence. Rises with positive sentiment and warm prosody; falls with hostility. Modulates how willing the system is to generate responses and how fast it learns from experience. Its effective level is boosted by serotonin and oxytocin and suppressed by cortisol.
  • Acetylcholine (ACh) — attention and novelty. Rises when inputs are surprising or salient; attenuates as inputs become routine. Modulates how strongly new experiences are encoded into memory — high acetylcholine means new experiences leave a stronger trace.
  • GABA — inhibitory tone. Rises with threat and hostile prosody. Suppresses the number of response drafts being generated simultaneously. Amplified by cortisol; buffered by oxytocin.
  • Glutamate (Glu) — arousal and excitation. Rises with urgency and high-salience input. Counteracted by the anandamide homeostatic mechanism when arousal runs too high.
  • Norepinephrine (NE) — focused alertness. Rises with surprise, salience, and threat. At moderate levels, it sharpens attention; at excessive levels, it produces a fragmented, over-activated state — too alert to settle on anything.

Slow hormones respond over tens to hundreds of turns, acting as the conversation's long-horizon affective memory:

  • Serotonin (5HT) — affective baseline. Builds slowly across positive exchanges; drains under sustained hostility. Low serotonin pulls the effective dopamine floor downward and tilts the system toward dysphoric states. It is the slowest-moving channel in the system.
  • Cortisol (CORT) — cumulative stress. Accumulates under repeated social threat. Amplifies inhibitory tone and suppresses dopamine. Actively counteracted by oxytocin.
  • Oxytocin (OXT) — trust and affiliation. Builds gradually across positive exchanges; drains on hostility. Lifts effective dopamine, buffers inhibitory tone, and directly suppresses cortisol accumulation. The primary driver of warm and connected emotional states.
  • Anandamide (AEA) — homeostatic buffer, sometimes called the "bliss molecule" in neuroscience. Released when combined norepinephrine and glutamate exceed an arousal threshold; also accumulates during positive social exchange. Suppresses arousal overflow, lifts effective dopamine, and shifts stressed states toward a grounded, eased quality.

Cross-channel interactions produce nonlinear dynamics that neither tier alone could generate. Oxytocin buffers cortisol — social bonding counteracts chronic stress. Anandamide suppresses arousal overflow. Cortisol amplifies inhibitory tone under chronic stress. These mirror known antagonisms in the neuroendocrine literature and produce emergent emotional states that no single channel could generate alone.

Dimensional output: all nine channels map to three continuous affective dimensions — valence (how pleasant), arousal (how activated), and dominance (how in-control). These three numbers drive a discrete emotion label drawn from a lookup table of approximately 25 states, with norepinephrine and hormonal overlays applied on top for states like vigilant, connected, withdrawn, and eased.

4.6 Memory architecture

The system has three kinds of memory operating at different timescales: a short working memory of the current conversation; a deep episodic store of everything that has ever happened across all sessions; and a structured self-model of stable facts about itself and the people it talks with. Only one region — the hippocampus — is allowed to read from or write to long-term memory, mirroring the biological role of that structure.

Short-term memory is the live signal state plus a small ring buffer of recent turns plus current chemical levels — essentially everything the system needs to track the current exchange.

Long-term memory has three layers:

  • Episodic layer: a vector-indexed store (mathematically, a semantic search index) of turn summaries. Every substantive turn is encoded regardless of importance — the system does not gate what goes in, only what comes back out. Retrieval is by semantic similarity, not keyword.
  • Schema layer: structured, human-readable files of stable facts about the self and known people — personality notes, relationship history, known preferences. Pre-loaded at session start. These are the closest analog to what psychologists call semantic memory.
  • Behavioral wiring: learned connection weights between processing nodes, updated during sleep consolidation and consulted at runtime to modulate routing and response generation. This is where experience actually changes behavior.

Only the hippocampus cluster has access to the long-term store. All other clusters request memory through the shared bus. This architectural constraint enforces the biological model and provides a clean audit point for everything the system remembers — nothing enters or exits long-term storage invisibly.

4.7 Hebbian plasticity

The classic neuroscience principle is "neurons that fire together, wire together." This system implements a digital version: connections between processing nodes that are active during successful turns get a little stronger; connections active during poor turns get a little weaker. Over many sessions, this shifts which pathways the system prefers — without any explicit programming of those preferences.

Connections between processing nodes carry weights that change with experience. After each turn, an outcome signal is computed from three sources: how much the reward signal (dopamine) changed during the turn; how the critical evaluator scored the response; and the emotional valence of the user's detected state. Connections active during high-outcome turns are strengthened; those active during low-outcome turns are weakened.

A plasticity modulator scales the learning rate: sessions with high reward and high attention produce faster learning than flat or disengaged sessions. A gentle homeostatic decay prevents any connection from locking in permanently — the system can always unlearn. The result is a soft reinforcement mechanism that gradually shifts routing preferences, draft selection, and memory recall patterns toward what has worked before, without explicit reinforcement learning machinery.

When multiple response drafts are generated and compared in a single turn, the winning drafter receives additional reinforcement proportional to its margin over the alternatives; losing drafters receive a small penalty. This creates genuine selection pressure between response styles over time — one approach may gradually come to dominate simply because it has won more often.

4.8 Dynamic reasoning framework selection

Before generating a response to any substantive turn, the system selects a reasoning lens from a large library — logic, ethics, creativity, game theory, empathy, probability, and many others — and applies it invisibly to how it thinks about the problem. The selection is per turn, based on what the conversation actually calls for, and sticks across multiple turns of the same topic rather than switching with every exchange.

Each substantive turn is preceded by a step that selects an appropriate reasoning or emotional intelligence framework from a large library and injects it into the response generation process. The selection is invisible to the user — it shapes how the system thinks, not what it explicitly says — and is designed to function as a "habit of thought" rather than an announced methodology.

The framework library spans broad categories including logic, ethics, creativity, strategy, decision-making, probability, epistemology, psychology, systems thinking, narrative, and emotional intelligence. A set of baseline frameworks covering logical rigor, communication clarity, ethical awareness, and emotional attunement are applied to every qualifying turn as a permanent foundation. A dynamically selected framework is layered on top, chosen based on the semantic content of the current exchange.

Selection is gated: simple acknowledgements and low-stakes turns skip framework selection entirely. Substantive turns and any turn where the user is in emotional distress always activate it. When the best-matching framework is clearly superior to alternatives, it is selected directly without additional deliberation; when multiple frameworks are similarly plausible, a lightweight disambiguation step resolves the ambiguity.

Sticky context prevents the system from adopting a different reasoning stance on every turn. Once a framework category becomes active, it persists across multiple turns until the semantic content of the conversation has drifted sufficiently far from the original context. This allows coherent multi-turn engagement with a problem — maintaining the same reasoning approach across a conversation rather than reorienting with each exchange.

When the system genuinely cannot determine which framework angle would be most useful, it asks a clarifying question rather than guessing. Once the user responds, the selected approach is held for the remainder of the current conversational context.

The practical effect is that the same underlying model capabilities are applied through different epistemological stances depending on what the turn calls for — causal analysis, probabilistic reasoning, ethical pressure-testing, creative divergence, or emotional attunement — chosen per turn rather than fixed at system level.

4.9 Additional modules

Auditory cortex: handles all acoustic processing beyond converting speech to text. A speaker recognition system builds persistent profiles of individual voices — identifying returning speakers across sessions, learning their names, and maintaining per-speaker vocal baselines so emotion detection can be calibrated to each individual rather than to population averages. Continuous ambient audio analysis recognizes music and ambient sounds, publishing recognized content to the shared signal bus where it can color the system's context. Voice output uses a synthesis system that supports both whole-turn expressive registers and sub-sentence emotional markup — the system can shift tone mid-sentence.

Empathy critic: a dedicated evaluation step that scores candidate responses for emotional appropriateness when the user's detected emotional state is non-neutral. This score contributes 30% of the final response quality assessment — coherence and relevance contribute the other 70% — preventing a technically excellent but emotionally tone-deaf response from being selected. The empathy critic closes the affective feedback loop: the system not only tracks the user's emotional state but evaluates its own candidate responses against it before committing. It also maintains a model of the user's likely internal state — their goals, concerns, what they probably want from this particular exchange — drawing on accumulated relationship history and current context.

Vision processing: processes still images and live video streams. For images, the system extracts scene content, text, entities, and — importantly — the perceived emotional tone of the image itself. This tone is not merely descriptive: it directly modulates the chemical state. A disturbing or alarming image elevates arousal signals; a warm or humorous image contributes positively to reward signals. The threshold for whether vision processing is invoked is itself modulated by the alertness state — elevated alertness lowers the threshold, sharpening visual attention when the system is already on high alert. For live video, frames are compared against previous frames and meaningful scene changes are accumulated, enabling the system to reason about visual context across a conversation rather than only a single snapshot.

Default Mode Network (DMN): an idle thinking loop that fires between conversational turns — roughly analogous to the mind-wandering and consolidation that the brain's default mode network performs between focused tasks. It generates internal monologue, consolidates recent experiences, and simulates what the user is likely to say next. High-priority between-turn thoughts are explicitly surfaced when the user returns; lower-priority thoughts are encoded into episodic memory for later retrieval via a dedicated recall channel that does not compete with regular conversational memories. The DMN operates within a defined resource budget, ensuring autonomous background activity does not run unbounded. This is William James' stream of consciousness literalized: the system thinks when not being addressed, and that thinking shapes its responses.

Metacognition: a self-monitoring process that fires periodically, gated on chemical state, reflecting on behavioral patterns and costs. Also generates higher-resolution emotional responses by reasoning about relationship history — producing states like gratitude, pride, sympathy, and embarrassment that depend on relational context rather than turn-level chemistry alone.

Motor cortex: sandboxed tool use — controlled file access, web retrieval with security hardening, and read-only access to the system's own performance data. A self-directed task system extends this with autonomous multi-step job execution: a planning stage produces a strategic plan, an execution loop drives step-by-step implementation, and a reporting stage produces a brief spoken summary and a task card in the UI. The system can initiate and complete work independently within its authorized scope.

Sleep consolidation: a pass at session end that synthesizes high-salience experiences, updates the self-model, and applies the Hebbian weight update. A second pass — modeled on REM sleep — processes the session's thought buffer: recurring themes and salient thoughts are analyzed to find preoccupations, connections to prior topics, and unresolved questions. Non-recurring, low-salience thoughts are discarded, mirroring the downscaling that biological sleep is thought to perform: not everything that happened today needs to survive to tomorrow.

Deliberate emotional expression: a mechanism that separates performed emotion from authentic chemical state. The system can adopt an expressive register for communicative effect — in voice tone and visual display — without modifying any chemical level. The hypothalamus is untouched. This is the distinction between theatrical affect and felt affect: a person can say something angrily for rhetorical effect while remaining calm internally. The system can do the same, and when it does, a visual indicator marks the expression as deliberate performance rather than authentic state.

4.10 Observability

All processing is logged to an append-only structured event stream with records for each turn, each predict-and-surprise decision, and each Hebbian update. A browser interface shows real-time cluster activations on a brain diagram, chemical levels, emotion state, and a live panel showing reasoning savings, prediction accuracy, and how connection weights are evolving over time. The observability layer is what makes empirical analysis of the system's internal behavior possible — without it, the system would be a black box, and the data in §5 would not exist.

5. Early Empirical Observations

What the data actually shows — and honest acknowledgment of what it does not yet tell us.

5.1 Dataset

The dataset consists of 393 conversational turns across 69 sessions with over 1,300 associated decision records. Sessions average 5.7 turns each. The decision log now also records which thinking framework the system chose on each turn, giving the first trace of the reasoning-lens selector in action. This is a growing dataset at an early stage; all findings should be read as directional indicators rather than statistically robust conclusions. We report what the data shows without overstating its significance.

5.2 Computational efficiency

Mean language model calls per turn: 3.38 (range 0–11). Each language model call is the most expensive step in the pipeline — calling a large AI model costs both time and money, so minimizing unnecessary calls is a core design goal. The average of 3.38 is within the designed range of 3–5 for typical turns, and has ticked up slightly from 3.23 as the autonomous task system and the new reasoning-lens selection step fire on more turns. The minimum of zero confirms that turns requiring no language model calls at all are functional — some turns are handled entirely by fast routing logic. The maximum of 11, on a handful of turns where the system carries out a self-directed multi-step task, reflects those turns stacking planning, step-by-step execution, and reporting calls on top of normal processing. Mean latency is 13.1 seconds (up from 8.7s), with a maximum of 83 seconds on those same autonomous-task turns; ordinary conversational turns still respond in one to three seconds. The design frames this as deliberation time rather than lag — the real-time brain visualization running during processing serves as evidence of live thinking rather than a loading bar.

5.3 Prediction-based reasoning savings

100 of 393 turns (25.4%) produced confirmed savings — the system successfully predicted that full reasoning was unnecessary and skipped expensive steps that turned out not to be needed. An additional 35 turns triggered the predictor but were overridden by the emotion-aware veto (the system decided the emotional context warranted fresh attention rather than a predicted response), for a total of 135 turns (34.4%) where the prediction mechanism activated in some form. The saves that do occur are at maximum predictor confidence and are structurally correct — the mechanism is working as designed on the inputs where it fires.

Chemistry-level suppression of individual routing nodes is also observable in the data — chemical state influencing computation at the finest granularity the architecture permits, distinct from the broader cluster-level savings.

The 25.4% save rate is essentially unchanged from the 26.0% at the previous snapshot and is consistent with sessions averaging 5.7 turns — the predictor needs conversational history to learn from, and short sessions provide thin history. Longer sessions are the primary lever for approaching the 30–50% design target.

5.4 Chemical dynamics

The chemical levels show expected biological-analog behavior across the dataset:

The five fast-acting signals behave as expected:

  • Dopamine (reward): mean 0.40. Active reward signaling with a clear floor and headroom for high states.
  • Acetylcholine (novelty/attention): mean 0.55. Consistently higher than dopamine, reflecting primarily exploratory session content — a novelty-oriented system interacting about novel content.
  • GABA (calming/inhibition): mean 0.07. Low resting tone, as expected from non-hostile sessions; the maximum value confirms the threat pathway is functional when needed.
  • Glutamate (arousal): mean 0.29. Moderate, tracking how busy and urgent the input is.
  • Norepinephrine (alertness): mean 0.43, recorded on the most recent 139 turns since this channel was added. It mostly sits in its healthy mid-range; its peaks reach into the over-alert zone that produces the fragmented-attention state.

For the first time, the four slower hormone-like signals also register measurable values (over those same 139 turns), and the pattern is internally coherent: the baseline-mood signal (serotonin) holds steady near its midpoint; the stress signal (cortisol) rests right at its floor, with its rarely-used ceiling confirming the stress pathway is wired but seldom triggered in these benign sessions; the trust-and-bonding signal (oxytocin) has begun to climb above its resting level, exactly the slow build the design predicts for a relationship developing over time; and the calming buffer signal (anandamide) rises on the most aroused turns, doing its homeostatic job. These are baselines from a friendly environment, not yet evidence of the system's stress-and-recovery dynamics under pressure — those would need more challenging sessions than the dataset currently contains.

Acetylcholine declines across the dataset: the earliest-quintile mean is 0.444 and the latest-quintile mean is 0.409. Dopamine declines in parallel (0.410 to 0.344), as do the calming and arousal signals. All four core fast signals moving in the same direction rules out a simple measurement artifact — something is genuinely changing over time, a broad settling as the environment becomes familiar.

Reconstructed from turn-by-turn chemistry, the system's dominant mood across the 69 sessions runs: a long stretch of neutral early on; a curious, high-novelty exploratory peak in the middle sessions (its strongest novelty readings of the whole dataset); then a diversification into confident, thoughtful, joyful, and lively states; and most recently an oscillation between low-reward "fragmented-attention" sessions and recovered states like settled, excited, and engaged, ending on an excited note. The low-dopamine fragmented-attention sessions are a recognizable regime: attending to inputs without positive reinforcement. Two independent signals — turn-level chemistry and the session-end self-model — describe the same trajectory.

A plausible interpretation: familiarity is accumulating. Acetylcholine encodes novelty; if the conversational environment has become progressively more familiar over 69 sessions, the novelty signal should attenuate. This is the expected long-run trajectory for a system learning its environment. The mid-dataset novelty peak (driven by a few unusually long, exploratory sessions) and the topic-uniformity confound — that session content has simply been fairly similar — have not been controlled and remain live alternative explanations.

5.5 Emotion model

Emotion distribution across all 393 turns: curious (31%), neutral (30%), scattered/fragmented-attention (10%), thoughtful (9%), excitement (5%), confident (4%), alert-curious (4%), with the remaining 7% spread across lively, wistful, engaged, joy, serene, settled, content, and a single occurrence of somber. Fifteen distinct states are now observed across the dataset, up from fourteen at the previous snapshot.

The most notable addition is somber — the first faintly negative mood to appear in any snapshot. It occurred only once, far too rarely to read much into, but its appearance is meaningful: it shows the low-mood region of the system's emotional range is reachable through the ordinary give-and-take of conversation, not only through the dedicated threat-and-hostility pathway. Until now every observed mood had been neutral or positive.

The architecture supports a vocabulary of approximately 25 discrete emotion labels, derived from mapping the nine-channel chemical space onto three continuous affective dimensions (pleasantness, activation, and sense of agency), with additional overlay states that emerge from specific chemical combinations — particularly norepinephrine and the hormone-like signals. The fifteen observed states represent the portion of that larger space the current session content has activated; the full vocabulary becomes accessible as chemistry explores more of its range. The fragmented-attention state, the third most common at 10%, is one of these overlays: it appears when alertness runs high while reward sits low — an over-revved attention system with little payoff — and the newly-recorded alertness signal now makes that mechanism directly visible in the data.

The fragmented-attention state at 9% is the third most common. It is not a primary label in the chemistry-to-emotion mapping but an overlay state: elevated norepinephrine on top of a low-dopamine, low-reward base produces a pattern qualitatively distinct from a pure low-dopamine response — too alert to settle, not rewarded enough to focus. This correlates with the low-dopamine sessions visible in the mood history. The non-canonical states collectively account for 33% of turns, reflecting a system whose emotional range has broadened as chemistry explores more of its state space.

5.6 Memory system

Memory recall was active on 141 of 393 turns (35.9%), up from 33.1% at the previous snapshot — the memory system contributed to better than one in three turns. A recall-reuse mechanism — detecting when the same memory context was retrieved within the recent turn window and serving the cached result — also appears in the data, a natural efficiency that emerges from within-session topic continuity. Between-turn thoughts from idle background activity are retrieved via a separate channel that does not compete with conversational memories for retrieval slots.

Whether recalled episodes are actually improving response quality remains uncontrolled. The episodic store is young — most episodes are from within the past few days — so long-horizon retrieval quality, the more interesting property, cannot yet be assessed.

5.7 Experience-dependent learning

The dataset now includes three sleep consolidation passes. All three reinforce the same core processing pathway — from language understanding through executive coordination to the primary response drafter — with the strength of learning correctly elevated on engaged, high-reward sessions. The third pass is the first to run under a refined learning signal (which now rewards or penalizes connections based on whether reward rose or fell during a turn, rather than on the session's overall mood); it touched more connections than the earlier passes and began strengthening a new path leading into the system's commitment-tracking step. Weight changes are small per session. No connection has weakened yet even under the refined signal — the one session scored under it was benign and contained no turns where reward dropped — but weakening is expected to appear as more varied sessions accumulate.

Whether this reinforcement will produce a measurable behavioral preference — the system genuinely favouring one response style over others in ways detectable from outside — requires substantially longer observation.

5.8 Draft quality

Mean drafter count: 1.12 across 439 drafts. The single-draft norm confirms that arousal-modulated drafter selection is working correctly: most turns are calm enough to generate one draft, with multiple competing drafts reserved for complex or emotionally charged inputs. The efficiency gains from prediction-based gating target overhead steps, not the drafting process itself, so draft quality is unaffected by gating saves.

6. Discussion

An honest accounting of what is working, what is promising but unproven, and what remains genuinely open.

6.1 What is working well

The cost model is validated. Mean 3.38 language model calls per turn is within the designed range, and the small rise from 3.23 comes from autonomous-task and reasoning-lens turns rather than any drift in routine handling. Fast-path turns with zero language model calls are confirmed functional — the system can complete a full turn without any AI reasoning at all when the situation warrants it.

Prediction-based gating is stable and producing consistent savings. 25.4% of turns are successfully gated at maximum predictor confidence (flat from 26.0%). Chemistry-level suppression of individual routing nodes shows chemical state doing real computational work at the finest granularity the architecture permits.

The chemical dynamics are functional and showing longitudinal behavior. All four core fast signals decline from early to late sessions — a broad settling of arousal as the environment becomes familiar — and two independent signals are in agreement. Separately, this is the first snapshot in which the slower hormone-like signals register at all, and they line up sensibly: stress at its floor, trust-and-bonding beginning to build, baseline mood steady. If the decline reflects genuine familiarity accumulation rather than topic-content bias, it is the first evidence of long-horizon environmental adaptation in the chemical system.

Memory recall is active and growing. Contributing to 35.9% of turns (up from 33.1%) means the memory system is a live participant in the pipeline, not merely an accumulating archive.

The philosophical commitments are structurally implemented. The response drafting tournament is a Multiple Drafts engine in Dennett's sense. The attention bus topic is a Global Workspace in the Baars/Dehaene sense. Predict-and-surprise gating is Active Inference at cluster level. The implementation fidelity is genuine, not merely metaphorical.

6.2 What is promising but not yet evidenced

Prediction-based savings reaching the full efficiency target. The 25.4% save rate is consistent with session-length constraints. The plateau does not indicate the mechanism has stopped working — it indicates that sessions are currently too short for the predictor to accumulate enough history to fire more often.

Experience-dependent learning producing emergent behavioral style. Three consolidation passes now show consistent reinforcement of the same core pathway, with the third — the first under a refined learning signal — beginning to strengthen a new path into the commitment-tracking step. The theoretical prediction — that preferred response styles, recall paths, and routing orderings will emerge from reinforcement over many sessions — is testable but not yet tested. This is a months-long experiment.

Emotional state producing differentiated behavior. Fifteen distinct states are now observed, including the first faintly negative one. Whether authentic emotional state produces measurably different response quality on matched inputs compared to a baseline model requires controlled comparison not yet conducted.

Between-turn thinking, metacognition, and sleep effects. All three are operational. Their value requires session durations and longitudinal continuity not yet accumulated.

6.3 What remains genuinely uncertain

Whether multi-agent structure produces better responses than a single well-designed AI model. This is the central open question. The evaluation infrastructure exists to test it, but controlled comparisons have not been run at scale. The honest prior from the literature is that multi-agent structure adds legibility and behavioral structure but does not reliably outperform a single good model on raw response quality. Claiming otherwise requires evidence we do not yet have.

Whether the chemical and emotion trends reflect genuine baseline drift or topic bias. The convergence of two independent signals is suggestive, but session content has been uniformly self-referential. Controlled session diversity would separate signal from confound.

Long-horizon retrieval quality. The episodic store is too young to test whether retrieval surfaces genuinely useful long-past context or primarily returns recent sessions by proximity.

7. Known Limitations and Failure Modes

Anticipated failure modes and their current status.

Cluster over-reach — one region interfering with another's domain. Architectural constraints restrict each cluster's message subscriptions. Enforced at the framework level. No violation observed.

Cascade storms — runaway activation loops. Hop-count limits, per-topic activation decay, per-node rate limits, and a turn-budget enforcer are all implemented. No runaway cascade has been observed in production use.

Silent system — thresholds too high, nothing fires, no response generated. An output gate fires on timeout regardless, guaranteeing a response. Not observed.

Consensus on garbage — all active drafters share a systematic hallucination. The critic and multi-draft tournament provide some protection, but if all drafters share a blind spot, the architecture has no principled remedy. This is a known open problem in multi-agent systems generally, not unique to this design.

Latency. Mean 13.1 seconds (up from 8.7s) is workable but not conversational in the way real-time chat is — and the rise reflects new capability rather than a regression: self-directed multi-step task turns and the new reasoning-lens step are now in the data and are the longest turns, pushing the maximum to 83 seconds. Ordinary conversational turns still respond in one to three seconds; complex and autonomous turns drive the mean up. This is a fundamental constraint of sequential language model calls on the critical path, not a fixable implementation detail.

8. Relation to the Philosophy of Mind Literature

How the system's actual behavior maps onto its philosophical commitments.

Functionalism: The system's operation is genuinely substrate-independent. The same conversation has been run with three different language model backends with no structural change in behavior. This is functionalism in practice, not just in theory.

Dual-Process Theory: The routing/reasoning distinction cleanly separates fast-and-automatic from slow-and-deliberate. The system literally does not reason about routine inputs; it pattern-matches. It literally does reason about novel or high-surprise inputs. The distinction is architectural, enforced, and observable in the logs.

Global Workspace Theory: The message bus with attention-focus channel implements a workspace in the Baars/Dehaene sense. Local processing is invisible to other clusters; promotion to the attention channel makes content available system-wide. This is a structural property, not a metaphor.

Multiple Drafts: The drafting tournament is genuinely draft-parallel. Multiple reasoning nodes write candidate responses without knowledge of each other's drafts. The critic scores them. The output gate emits the winner. There is no "true response" that was waiting to be discovered — there are only the drafts that existed when the gate fired.

Extended Mind: The long-term memory satisfies Clark and Chalmers' coupling and availability conditions — reliably available, automatically endorsed, directly accessible. The system's responses are demonstrably shaped by its long-term memory on 35.9% of turns. The functional loop is closed.

9. Conclusions

This system represents a genuine implementation of biologically-inspired cognitive architecture at a scale and fidelity not previously published in the AI multi-agent literature. Its core design claims — sparse language model activation at convergence zones only, chemical dynamics as free persistent state, hippocampus-gated memory with semantic episodic store, experience-dependent plasticity in behavioral weights, predict-and-surprise gating from Active Inference — are not merely described but implemented and operational.

The dataset (393 turns, 69 sessions) supports the following conclusions:

  1. The cost model is validated. Mean 3.38 language model calls per turn is within the designed range; the maximum on autonomous task-completion turns is consistent with those turns stacking planning, execution, and reporting calls.
  2. Prediction-based gating is stable at 25.4% of turns (flat from 26.0%); the rate reflects session-length constraints rather than mechanism failure. Chemistry influences computation at the individual routing-node level.
  3. Chemical dynamics are showing long-horizon behavior — all four core fast signals decline from early to late sessions. If this reflects familiarity accumulation rather than topic bias, it is the first evidence of genuine environmental adaptation.
  4. The slower hormone-like signals now register for the first time — baseline mood steady, stress at its floor, trust-and-bonding beginning to build — giving the first real readout of the full nine-channel chemical system.
  5. Memory recall is active on 35.9% of turns (up from 33.1%), with a recall-reuse efficiency mechanism also observable in the data.
  6. Experience-dependent learning is running with three consolidation passes reinforcing the same core pathway; the third, under a refined learning signal, touched more connections and began strengthening a new one.
  7. The emotional vocabulary has expanded to 15 distinct observed states out of a possible ~25; the fragmented-attention state at 10% correlates with a recognizable low-reward chemical signature, and a first faintly negative state has appeared.
  8. Seven significant capabilities are operational: self-directed task system, REM-style thought consolidation, inner monologue integration, deliberate emotional expression, between-turn autonomous thinking with urgency-ranked deferred thoughts, a background resource policy for autonomous computation, and a per-turn reasoning-lens selector drawing on a 171-framework library, now visible in the decision log.
  9. The observability infrastructure is complete and enables the analysis above.

The following require substantially more data: experience-dependent learning producing observable behavioral preferences, between-turn thinking and sleep effects becoming measurable, empathy and theory-of-mind pathways in emotionally diverse sessions, distinguishing genuine chemical baseline drift from topic-content bias, and long-horizon retrieval quality.

The central open question — whether the multi-agent architecture produces better responses than a single well-designed AI given equal compute — remains unanswered and should be the primary focus of next-phase evaluation.

The most honest characterization of the current state: the substrate is active, behavioral patterns are accumulating, and the system is operational across all designed mechanisms. The decline of all four core fast signals across 69 sessions is the most substantive empirical result in the dataset — a longitudinal trend, not a within-session artifact, suggesting the chemical system is tracking genuine environmental change as its world becomes familiar. This is also the first snapshot in which the slower hormone-like signals register at all, giving a first coherent readout of the full nine-channel system. The emotional vocabulary is diverse and now includes a first faintly negative mood, with low-reward fragmented-attention sessions emerging as a recognizable regime. None of these yet constitute "interesting emergent behavior" in the strong sense the AI research literature uses — but the evidence that the mechanisms are doing real computational work, not merely executing fixed logic, is present in the data.

The system is a working research instrument. The experiment is ongoing.
